Inclusive Smart Cities and Digital Health

An event hosted by IPAL and the Institut Français
2nd & 3rd February 2015, Singapore

Promote research mobility

To encourage mobility among faculty members, tenured and young researchers, doctoral students and students in master degrees with an interest towards research. This mobility between French and Singaporean institutes is facilitated through IPAL laboratory and its partners.

Strengthen scientific collaborations

To strengthen scientific collaborations between the two countries through joint applications to national research projects in France (e.g. ANR, FUI, Investissements d’avenir) and Singapore (e.g. A*STAR, NRF), or internationally (e.g. EU, ITEA, STIC Asia, BIO Asia).

Increase technology transfer

To develop opportunities of transfer for innovative technologies into the growing Asian markets with the support of French and European companies based in Singapore, while leveraging and supporting Singapore's flourishing network of high-standard SMEs.

David Rosenblum

Known unknowns: testing in the presence of uncertainty

Biography

David S. Rosenblum is Dean of the School of Computing at the National University of Singapore and a Professor in the Department of Computer Science. He also directs the Felicitous Computing Institute and is a member of the NUS Graduate School for Integrative Sciences and Engineering.
He received his PhD in 1988 from the Department of Electrical Engineering at Stanford University, and he did his undergraduate work at North Texas State University (now the University of North Texas). From 1988 to 1996 he was a Member of the Technical Staff at AT&T Bell Laboratories (now AT&T Labs Research) in Murray Hill, NJ. After leaving Bell Labs, from 1996 to 2001 he was on the Faculty of the Department (now School) of Information and Computer Science at the University of California, Irvine. From 2001 to 2003 he was on leave from UC Irvine working as Chief Technology Officer and Principal Architect of PreCache, a startup company developing technology in the area of publish/subscribe networking. And from 2004 to 2011 he was Professor of Software Systems in the Department of Computer Science at University College London.
Professor Rosenblum's research has addressed a wide range of problems spanning the breadth of the software development life cycle, including software specification, architecture, design, testing, analysis and maintenance, as well as problems underlying the processes that guide large software projects. His current research focuses on the scalability of architectures for large-scale software systems; probabilistic verification of software systems; and the design and validation of mobile, context-aware adaptive systems for ubiquitous computing. In 1997 he received a CAREER Award from the US National Science Foundation for his work on distributed component-based software, and from 2004-2009 he held a Wolfson Research Merit Award from the Royal Society.

Navigation and Control of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles in GPS-Denied Environments

Visit Ben M. Chen's homepage here.

Keynote Abstract

Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) have made huge progress in military and civilian missions, ranging from surveillance, reconnaissance, to logistics and transportation. Particularly, to meet requirements of high-demand applications, it is necessary to develop advanced unmanned systems that are capable of autonomously navigating in cluttered indoor and outdoor GPS-denied environments, such as hostile buildings, radiation contaminated areas and forest.
In this talk, we aim to report some recent research activities related to GPS-less navigation and control of UAVs conducted by the UAV Research Group at National University of Singapore. Topics on the development of micro aerial vehicles, unconventional aircraft, multiple UAVs formation and some ongoing research projects will also be highlighted.

Personal Information Search and Discovery

Her research interests are in Personal Information Management, Ranked Query Processing, Semi-structured data and Web data Management. Amélie received her Ph.D. in Computer Science from Columbia University in 2005. From March 1999 to August 2000, Amélie was a member of the VERSO project at INRIA-Rocquencourt. She received B.S. and M.S. degrees from Université Paris Dauphine, France in 1998 and 1999, respectively. She is the recipient of a Microsoft Live Labs Award (2006), three Google Research Awards (2008, 2010, and 2012) and an NSF CAREER award (2009).

Visit Amélie Marian's homepage here.

Keynote Abstract

Personal data is now pervasive as digital devices are capturing every part of our lives. Data is constantly collected and saved by users, either voluntarily in files, emails, social media interactions, multimedia objects, calendar items, contacts, etc., or passively by various applications such as GPS tracking of mobile devices, records of utility usage, financial transactions, or quantified self sensors. Everywhere users go, everything they do, they leave a digital trace that acts as a digital memory of their past actions, interactions, and whereabouts. Having a personal “memex,” a digital system to supplement one’s memory, as envisioned by Vannevar Bush last century, is now close to possible. In this talk, I will discuss new challenges and applications of Personal Information Management.  I will present a unified and intuitive data model to represent personal information, which has multiple provenance and native formats. This data model is used as the foundation for intuitive search tools that rely on contextual cues to find past information, as well as for personal knowledge extraction techniques that leverage individual personal data and query patterns.

Colin L. Stewart

Towards an Understanding of the Functional Architecture of the Cells Nucleus in Development, Aging and Disease

Biography

Colin Stewart received his DPhil from the University of Oxford where he developed some of the key techniques currently used to genetically modify mice. Following postdoctoral work in Germany with Rudolf Jaenisch and then at EMBL he moved to the USA where he worked at the Roche Institute of Molecular Biology and subsequently at the National Cancer Institute. Since 2007 he has been Research and Assistant director at the Institute of Medical Biology and the Singapore Biopolis. His research interests have largely centered on the genetics of mammalian development and disease and currently his principal focus is on a collection of diseases the laminopathies, all of which arise from mutations in 1 gene. The laminopathies are providing insights into cardiovascular disease, obesity and ageing. These diseases are modelled in mice and using patient specific iPS cells. The molecular basis to these diseases is currently being investigated using the novel proteomic technique BioID to define the nuclear protein interactome and how it is disrupted by the laminopathies.

Visit Colin L. Stewart's homepage here.

Keynote Abstract

The cell’s nucleus in the physical repository of the genome. For many years the nucleus has been considered as being a porous bag into which proteins would enter to regulate gene expression and message RNA would exit to produce new proteins. However over the past decade our view on nuclear structure and its relevance to nuclear function has become more sophisticated. For instance, considerable efforts are being made to investigate whether the physical position of a specific gene within the nucleus is important to its expression. In addition, the nucleus is now known to contain many sub-nuclear structures whose function is being elucidated. Among these is the nuclear lamina and envelope, structures that function as the physical boundary between the nucleus and the rest of the cell. Interest in the lamina was stimulated by the discovery that some 8, albeit rare diseases, are caused by mutations in 1 structural protein- lamin A. However these rare diseases in turn are providing insight into diseases that are of major importance to world health, particularly aging, obesity and cardiovascular disease. Just how 1 such protein that is widely expressed, results in such tissue specific diseases is of importance since in suggests that the physical organization of the nucleus is of much greater significance and sophistication than in just regulating gene expression. In this post-genomic era, we are using a newly developed technique, BioID, to identify and catalogue interactions between the lamin proteins and other nuclear proteins to define the nuclear interactome i.e. how all nuclear proteins interact with each other, which are the key “nodal” proteins and how disruption to these interactions leads to disease.
